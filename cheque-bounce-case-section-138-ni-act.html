Cheque Bounce Case – Complete Legal Guide

Understanding Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act

← Back to Legal Insights

Cheque bounce disputes are among the most common financial litigation matters in India. The law governing cheque dishonour is contained under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881.

This provision was introduced to strengthen the credibility of banking transactions and prevent misuse of cheques in financial dealings.

When a cheque issued towards repayment of debt is dishonoured due to insufficient funds or other valid reasons, the drawer of the cheque may face criminal liability under the law.

What is a Cheque Bounce Case?

A cheque bounce occurs when a bank refuses to honour a cheque presented for payment. This usually happens due to insufficient funds, account closure, or signature mismatch.

If the cheque was issued to repay a legally enforceable debt or liability, the payee can initiate legal proceedings under Section 138 NI Act.

Common Reasons for Cheque Dishonour

  • Insufficient funds in bank account
  • Account closed by the drawer
  • Signature mismatch
  • Payment stopped by the drawer
  • Cheque validity expired

Legal Procedure in Cheque Bounce Cases

The law provides a specific procedure that must be followed before filing a complaint in court.

Step 1: Cheque Presentation
The cheque must be presented to the bank within its validity period (usually 3 months).

Step 2: Dishonour Memo
If the cheque is dishonoured, the bank issues a return memo stating the reason.

Step 3: Legal Notice
The payee must send a legal notice to the drawer within 30 days demanding payment.

Step 4: 15-Day Payment Window
The drawer gets 15 days to clear the payment after receiving the notice.

Step 5: Filing Criminal Complaint
If payment is not made within 15 days, a criminal complaint can be filed before the Magistrate Court within 30 days.

Punishment for Cheque Bounce

Under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, the court may impose:

  • Imprisonment up to 2 years
  • Fine up to twice the cheque amount
  • Or both imprisonment and fine

Important Legal Defences

The accused may raise certain defences depending on the facts of the case:

  • Cheque issued as security
  • No legally enforceable debt
  • Cheque misused or stolen
  • Notice not properly served

Conclusion

Cheque bounce litigation plays a crucial role in protecting financial transactions in India. Strict compliance with legal timelines and procedures is essential for both complainants and accused persons.

Seeking proper legal guidance ensures that rights are protected and the case proceeds efficiently before the court.
