What To Do If Police Call You for Inquiry?

By Nikhil Chugh , Advocate | Supreme Court Bar Association Member | Panipat District Court

Receiving a call from the police can cause anxiety for many people. However, not every police inquiry means that you are accused of a crime. Police may call individuals for clarification, witness statements, or preliminary investigation.

Understanding your legal rights and responding appropriately can help protect you from unnecessary legal complications.

1. Do Not Panic

A police inquiry call does not automatically mean that you are guilty of any offence. Often the police are simply gathering information related to an investigation.

2. Ask Basic Details Politely

Before visiting the police station, it is advisable to ask:

  • Name and designation of the police officer
  • Name of the police station
  • Purpose of the inquiry
  • Whether you are being called as a witness or suspect

3. Are You Legally Bound to Visit the Police Station?

Under Indian law, police cannot arbitrarily detain or arrest individuals without legal justification. If you receive a written notice requiring your presence for investigation, you are generally expected to comply with that notice.

Such notices are issued as part of the investigation procedure to ensure cooperation without unnecessary arrest.

4. Right to Legal Representation

You have the right to consult a lawyer before making any statement to the police. Informing your advocate before appearing for inquiry can help ensure that your legal rights are protected during questioning.

5. Do Not Sign Documents Without Understanding

Never sign any document, statement, or blank paper without reading and understanding its contents. If necessary, seek legal advice before signing any document during an inquiry.

6. Special Protections for Women

Women enjoy additional legal safeguards during police procedures. In general circumstances, women should not be called to the police station late at night and questioning should follow specific procedural protections.

7. When Should You Seek Legal Help?

If the police threaten arrest without clear explanation, pressure you to confess, or attempt to record statements improperly, you should immediately seek legal assistance.

In appropriate cases, remedies such as anticipatory bail may be available to protect your liberty.

Conclusion

Cooperating with lawful police investigation is important, but citizens must also be aware of their legal rights. Understanding the difference between an inquiry and an accusation helps individuals respond calmly and responsibly during police interactions.
